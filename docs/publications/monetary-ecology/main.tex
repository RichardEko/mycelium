\begin{abstract}
Money is not neutral: a monetary architecture encodes, in its rules, who is
counted, who decides, and who claims the surplus it generates. We introduce a
framework for evaluating monetary architectures along three orthogonal axes---
\emph{Moral Circle Breadth} (MCB: who counts), \emph{Polycentricity} (P: who
decides), and \emph{Surplus-Claim Regime} (S: who benefits)---together with an
\emph{Information-Rights} diagnostic ($\Iax$: privacy for people, transparency for
power) that gates the three so that good distributional outcomes cannot be claimed
through opaque or capturable means. The axes are orthogonal as \emph{measurements}:
improving one does not automatically improve another. They are not, however,
causally independent: we argue a structural tendency, $P \rightsquigarrow S$, by
which low polycentricity drifts toward concentrated surplus-claim, because absent
distributed and contestable power, coordination dynamics tend toward rent. We give
a 0--5 scoring rubric, plot systems on the $(P, S)$ plane with MCB as magnitude and
$\Iax$ as a confidence qualifier, and apply the framework to a range of monetary
configurations, from sovereign fiat and authoritarian central-bank digital currency
through platform finance to a plural, federated ``monetary ecology'' built on
complex-adaptive-systems principles. We close by mapping MCB/P/S/$\Iax$ onto the
three-layer model of a coordinator-free substrate, showing that the monetary case is
an instance of the same structural argument developed for distributed
computing~\cite{nicholson2026,nicholson2026hlks}: where a coordinator concentrates
knowledge it cannot possess, a surplus-claim regime concentrates value it did not
solely produce, and the corrective in both domains is local resolution, plural
power, and exit.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A monetary system is usually evaluated on narrow technical criteria: price
stability, transaction throughput, settlement finality. These matter, but they are
silent on the questions a monetary architecture in fact answers in its design.
Every monetary system encodes, in its rules of issuance, custody, and redemption, a
set of distributive and political commitments: whose interests it protects, how
decision rights over it are distributed, and who claims the residual value it
generates. Money is not neutral. A system can be perfectly stable and perfectly
extractive; it can settle in milliseconds and answer to no one.

This paper introduces a framework for making those commitments explicit and
comparable. It evaluates a monetary architecture on three orthogonal axes---who is
counted, who decides, who benefits---and adds an information-rights diagnostic that
prevents a system from claiming good outcomes through opaque means. The framework is
comparative rather than predictive: it does not forecast which system will prevail,
but renders the structural commitments of competing designs legible on a common
scale.

The motivating contrast is between two failure modes. A monetary
\emph{monoculture}---a single centralised system, whether sovereign fiat, an
authoritarian central-bank digital currency, or a closed financial platform---is
brittle and concentrates both decision rights and surplus. We use the deliberately
sharp term \emph{blood money} for the limit case: a medium that finances outcomes
its holders would not individually endorse---through hidden subsidy, externalised
cost, and the passivity that centralised issuance induces. The alternative is a
monetary \emph{ecology}: a plural, multi-layered, federated system designed on
complex-adaptive-systems (CAS) principles, which appears untidy but is adaptive,
resilient, and corrigible under stress. The framework's purpose is to make the
difference between these architectures measurable rather than rhetorical.

\paragraph{Contributions.} This paper:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Defines the MCB/P/S framework---three orthogonal axes for evaluating a
  monetary architecture---and the $\Iax$ information-rights diagnostic that gates it
  (\S\ref{sec:framework}, \S\ref{sec:ihat}).
\item Distinguishes \emph{measurement orthogonality} (the axes vary independently)
  from \emph{structural tendency}, and argues the directional relation
  $P \rightsquigarrow S$: concentrated power drifts toward concentrated
  surplus-claim (\S\ref{sec:causality}).
\item Gives a 0--5 scoring method and applies it to a range of monetary
  configurations, from fiat and CBDC to a federated monetary ecology
  (\S\ref{sec:scoring}, \S\ref{sec:application}).
\item Maps MCB/P/S/$\Iax$ onto the three-layer model of a coordinator-free
  substrate, exhibiting the monetary case as an instance of the same structural
  argument made for distributed computing (\S\ref{sec:substrate}).
\item States the framework's scope and limits honestly (\S\ref{sec:limits}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{The MCB/P/S Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

The framework rests on three questions a monetary architecture cannot avoid
answering, and which conventional metrics do not ask.

\begin{definition}[Moral Circle Breadth (MCB) --- ``who counts?'']
The effective scope of those granted protection, voice, and material consideration
by the system. A broad moral circle extends standing beyond present citizens to
non-citizens, future generations, non-human animals, and ecosystems.
\end{definition}

MCB has a load-bearing \emph{voiceless edge}. The parties whose standing most tests
a moral circle---future generations, non-human animals, ecosystems---are precisely
those that cannot advocate for themselves within the system. A monetary architecture
extends them standing only structurally: through rules (provisioning for the future,
internalising ecological cost) rather than through their participation, because they
cannot participate. MCB therefore measures not who is represented but who is
\emph{considered}, and its hardest cases are those with no voice to withdraw.

\begin{definition}[Polycentricity (P) --- ``who decides?'']
The dispersion, contestability, and recallability of decision rights over the system
across multiple independent centres---municipalities, councils, co-operatives,
protocols---rather than their concentration in a single authority.
\end{definition}

Polycentricity, in the sense developed by Ostrom~\cite{ostrom1990} and her
predecessors, is not merely the existence of many centres but their
\emph{contestability and recallability}: decision rights that can be challenged and
withdrawn, not merely distributed and then entrenched.

\begin{definition}[Surplus-Claim Regime (S) --- ``who benefits?'']
The incidence of residual income and rents---who captures the value a system
generates after costs are met. A distributed surplus-claim regime returns value to
workers, communities, and the commons; a concentrated one routes it to owners of the
issuing or custodial layer.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Orthogonality}

The three axes are \emph{orthogonal as measurements}: improving one does not
automatically improve another, and a system may score high on one while low on
another. Broad refugee protections (MCB high) can coexist with pervasive
surveillance (P low). A widely distributed shareholder base (an apparent P) can
still concentrate surplus-claim (S low). It is precisely because the axes can
diverge that measuring them separately is informative: a single ``goodness'' score
would hide the trade-offs that distinguish architectures. The framework's
descriptive content is the \emph{vector} $(\text{MCB}, P, S)$, not its average.

Each axis draws on an established tradition---the moral-circle literature after
Singer~\cite{singer1981} for MCB, Ostrom's~\cite{ostrom1990} polycentric governance
for P, and the political economy of surplus and rent for S---but the synthesis into
a single three-axis evaluative instrument for monetary architecture is, to our
knowledge, novel to this work.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Measurement Orthogonality Is Not Causal Independence: \texorpdfstring{$P \rightsquigarrow S$}{P leads to S}}
\label{sec:causality}

That the axes are orthogonal as measurements is sometimes mistaken for the claim
that they are causally independent. They are not. We argue a directional structural
tendency, written $P \rightsquigarrow S$ to mark it as a tendency rather than a law:

\begin{quote}
Low polycentricity drifts toward concentrated surplus-claim. Where decision rights
over a monetary system concentrate, and absent explicit anti-rent rules and
interoperability, coordination dynamics tend toward monopoly and oligopoly, and the
surplus-claim regime concentrates with them.
\end{quote}

The mechanism is the one Olson~\cite{olson1965} identified: the benefits of capturing
a coordination mechanism are concentrated on the captor while its costs are diffuse,
so concentrated power is under standing pressure to convert itself into concentrated
claim. Polycentric, contestable power is the counterweight: distributed and recallable
decision centres constitute a buffer between individuals and mega-institutions that
constrains both state monopoly and private capture, and so tends to keep both the
moral circle broad and the surplus-claim regime distributed.

The relation is directional and probabilistic, not deterministic. High P does not
\emph{guarantee} distributed S---a polycentric system can still tolerate rent---but
low P reliably \emph{erodes} it. This asymmetry is why the framework treats P and S
as distinct measurements while acknowledging the tendency between them: one must
measure S directly, because P does not determine it; but a low P score is a leading
indicator that S is under structural pressure. The corollary for design is that
distributing power is necessary but not sufficient for distributing surplus---the
anti-rent rules must be written explicitly, not assumed to follow.

\section{The Information-Rights Diagnostic \texorpdfstring{($\Iax$)}{(I-hat)}}
\label{sec:ihat}

A system can score well on MCB, P, and S while achieving those outcomes through
mechanisms that are opaque, unverifiable, or quietly capturable---in which case the
scores are claims rather than facts. The $\Iax$ diagnostic guards against this. Its
principle is \emph{privacy for people, transparency for power}: individuals should be
shielded from surveillance, while the mechanisms that exercise power over the system
should be continuously auditable.

$\Iax$ is a \emph{red-flag diagnostic}, not a fourth axis. It does not describe a
distributional commitment; it qualifies the confidence with which the other three can
be asserted. We compute it as a weighted score over five domains, with penalties for
specific opacity failures:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Proofs of reserves and flows} (25\%): continuous cryptographic proofs
  (5) versus none (0).
\item \emph{Portability and exit} (25\%): frictionless portability of data and value
  (5) versus high lock-in (0).
\item \emph{Custody topology} (20\%): federated, multi-party custody (5) versus a
  single third-party custodian (0).
\item \emph{Governance traceability} (20\%): public, verifiable decision logs (5)
  versus opaque off-chain governance (0).
\item \emph{Surveillance exposure} (10\%): privacy-by-default circulation (5) versus
  full transaction surveillance (0).
\end{itemize}

Penalties are deducted for material failures: no public proofs ($-1.0$); a single
custodian able to override ($-1.0$); a failed portability test ($-0.5$); gag clauses
that block audit ($-0.5$); a material incident unreported beyond thirty days
($-0.5$); opaque platform dependency ($-0.5$). The diagnostic is then
\[
  \Iax = \max\!\bigl(0,\ \min(5,\ \Iax_{\text{raw}} - \textstyle\sum \text{penalties})\bigr),
\]
and reported \emph{alongside} the MCB/P/S vector as a confidence qualifier: a high
distributional score carried by a low $\Iax$ is provisional, because the means by
which it is achieved cannot be verified and may be captured without notice.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Scoring Method}
\label{sec:scoring}

Each axis is scored on a 0--5 scale as a weighted average of two to five observable
indicators, normalised to the scale. Representative indicators are: for MCB, asylum
and migration standing, climate and intergenerational provisioning, non-human
standing; for P, recallability of authority, the scope of genuinely local decision,
press and association freedom; for S, labour's share of value, the legal standing of
co-operatives and the commons, the strength of antitrust, the progressivity of the
claim on surplus. Indicator sets are domain-specific and are published with each
score so that a placement can be contested at the level of its evidence, not merely
its conclusion---an application of the framework's own polycentricity to itself.

Systems are plotted on the plane $(x = P,\ y = S)$ with bubble magnitude given by MCB
and $\Iax$ reported separately as a confidence-qualified annotation. The plane is
chosen deliberately: P and S are the axes between which the $P \rightsquigarrow S$
tendency runs, so a system's position relative to the diagonal---power distributed
but surplus concentrated, or the reverse---is the most diagnostic single view, and
MCB-as-magnitude shows whether the system's gains are extended broadly or narrowly.

Two readings stand out. First, the configurations that concentrate decision rights
(low P) are the same ones that concentrate surplus-claim (low S)---the
$P \rightsquigarrow S$ tendency made visible. Authoritarian CBDC and platform finance
sit in the low-$(P,S)$ corner not by coincidence but because centralised issuance
and custody place both the decision and the residual claim in the same hands.
Second, no single configuration dominates on all axes; the framework surfaces
genuine trade-offs (a market-forward design scoring high on P and S may extend a
narrower moral circle than a commons-forward one) rather than collapsing them into a
ranking.

\subsection{The High-Scoring Region: A Monetary Ecology}

The configurations that score broadly across the axes share a structural form: they
are \emph{plural and layered} rather than monolithic. A representative architecture is
a recursive stack---minimal federation invariants at the base (exit rights, proofs of
reserves, common formats); a neutral settlement and reserve layer; a privacy-
preserving community circulation layer under rotating, multi-party custody; and a
bottom-up mutual-credit layer---tuned per community through published parameters
rather than imposed by a centre. Such a design scores high on P by construction
(decision rights are local, contestable, and recallable), supports high S through
explicit anti-rent rules (demurrage, quadratic funding, distributed custody), and
admits a broad MCB because communities may extend standing as their charters direct.
Crucially, it scores high on $\Iax$: federated custody and public proofs make capture
visible and costly. We summarise this architecture here as the framework's worked
high-scoring case; its full design and an implementation path are developed in the
originating essay and are beyond the scope of this evaluative paper.

The decisive structural feature is \emph{exit}: because participants and communities
can leave a configuration they reject---taking value and state with them---no layer
can hold them against the threat of departure. Exit, in Hirschman's~\cite{hirschman1970}
sense, is what makes the plurality corrigible rather than merely diverse: ``better a
pluralism you can leave than a monoculture you cannot.''

\section{The Monetary Case as a Substrate-Design Instance}
\label{sec:substrate}

The framework was developed for monetary architecture, but its structure is not
specific to money. The companion papers argue that coordinator-based architectures
fail structurally across domains~\cite{nicholson2026}, and that coordinator-free,
locally-resolved substrate design is the unique epistemically correct response
wherever knowledge is heterogeneous and locally held~\cite{nicholson2026hlks}. The
monetary case is an instance of that argument, and MCB/P/S/$\Iax$ maps onto the
three-layer model of a coordinator-free substrate:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Polycentricity} is the monetary form of the substrate's coordinator-free
  layer: decision rights resolved locally and contestably, not aggregated at a
  centre that cannot possess the local knowledge the decision requires. Where a
  coordinator concentrates knowledge it cannot possess, a centralised monetary
  authority concentrates monetary decisions it cannot rightly make for the localities
  it governs.
\item \emph{Surplus-Claim Regime} is the monetary form of the capture problem: where
  a coordinator becomes a chokepoint that extracts coordination rent, a concentrated
  surplus-claim regime extracts value the issuing layer did not solely produce. The
  $P \rightsquigarrow S$ tendency is the monetary statement of the same risk the
  capture argument identifies for substrates generally.
\item \emph{Information Rights} ($\Iax$) is the monetary form of the substrate's
  legibility and exit requirements: proofs of reserves, portability, and federated
  custody are, in monetary dress, the transparency-and-exit mechanisms by which a
  coordinator-free system makes capture visible and bounded.
\item \emph{Moral Circle Breadth} is the axis with the least direct substrate
  analogue, and marks where the monetary domain carries an explicitly normative
  commitment---who the system is \emph{for}---that the computing substrate leaves to
  its operators.
\end{itemize}

That the same structural prescription---local resolution, plural and contestable
power, exit, and transparency of mechanism---arises independently in monetary design
and in distributed computing is, we argue elsewhere~\cite{nicholson2026hlks}, not an
analogy but a convergence: both are instances of coordinating heterogeneous local
knowledge without a centre that can possess it.

\section{Scope and Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Comparative, not predictive.} The framework renders structural
  commitments legible and comparable; it does not forecast which architecture will
  prevail, which depends on adoption dynamics the framework does not model.
\item \emph{Scores are indicative.} The 0--5 placements are comparative judgements
  over published indicators, not precise measurements; their purpose is to be
  contested at the indicator level. Whether any axis admits a quantification stable
  across very different monetary systems is open.
\item \emph{Tendency, not law.} The $P \rightsquigarrow S$ relation is a directional
  structural tendency, argued informally; its strength and boundary conditions are
  open empirical questions.
\item \emph{Normative-adjacency.} The framework keeps a structural claim (which
  architectures distribute power and surplus, and make their mechanisms legible)
  separable from the normative one (which distributions are just). It measures the
  commitments a design encodes; it does not, by itself, adjudicate between them.
\item \emph{The architecture is a proposal.} The high-scoring monetary ecology
  summarised in \S\ref{sec:application} is a design proposal, not an established
  result; this paper's contribution is the evaluative framework, not a guarantee of
  any particular architecture's success.
\end{enumerate}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

Money is never neutral; a monetary architecture answers, in its rules, who counts,
who decides, and who benefits, and conventional metrics leave those answers
implicit. The MCB/P/S framework makes them explicit and comparable, the $\Iax$
diagnostic ensures that good distributional outcomes are not claimed through opaque
or capturable means, and the $P \rightsquigarrow S$ tendency names the structural
pressure by which concentrated power becomes concentrated claim. Applied to money,
the framework distinguishes brittle, extractive monocultures from plural, corrigible
ecologies; mapped onto substrate design, it shows the monetary case to be an instance
of a more general structural result. The framework does not prescribe a single
architecture. It makes the difference between architectures measurable---which is the
precondition for choosing among them on grounds more honest than throughput.
